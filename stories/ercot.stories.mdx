    ## Overview
    Texas operates a largely independent power grid through the Electric Reliability Council of Texas (ERCOT), serving approximately 90% of the state‚Äôs electric load. This isolation, combined with limited cold and heat weatherization, makes the ERCOT grid uniquely vulnerable to extreme weather events. In recent years, Texans have experienced firsthand how both deep freezes and miserable heat waves can stress this grid to its limits. With an increasing population, understanding the dual threats of cold and heat stress is essential for building a more resilient energy future for the state. A couple historical extremes that have tested the ERCOT grid have made national headlines, causing plenty of exposure to this problem, but implementing better mitigation practices remains a priority to sustain the ERCOT grid.
  </Prose>

    ## February 2021 Deep Freeze
    Between February 10‚Äì27, 2021, Texas faced one of its most catastrophic weather events in modern history. Dubbed ‚ÄúThe Great Texas Freeze,‚Äù Winter Storm Uri brought prolonged subfreezing temperatures, heavy snow, and ice that crippled infrastructure statewide. Every county in Texas was under a Winter Storm Warning during the Valentine's Day storm, a rare event for a state better known for its summer heat. At the storm's peak, official counts place over 4.5 million customers without power, but Eagle-I data reports the number reached as high as 5.2 million. This is out of 14.9 million customers tracked in the state. According to ERCOT, the grid came within just four minutes and 37 seconds of total collapse, which could have led to a blackout lasting weeks or even months.
  </Prose>

    ## Recent Summer Heat Waves
    While Winter Storm Uri made national headlines, extreme heat has also tested the ERCOT grid in recent years. In the summers of 2022-2024, Texas experienced persistent heat waves with record-breaking temperatures, pushing electricity demand to all-time highs. Unlike in the winter, where supply failures dominate headlines, the summer challenge is demand-driven. As air conditioners ramp up across millions of homes, the strain on generation and transmission infrastructure becomes enormous. On multiple days, ERCOT issued conservation appeals to customers, urging residents to reduce electricity use during peak afternoon hours to avoid rolling blackouts.

    In August 2023, ERCOT recorded an all-time peak demand of 85,508 megawatts, indicating that the grid is operating near its limits. Just one year later in August of 2024, the all-time peak demand record was broken again, with a peak demand of 85,558.98 megawatts being recorded. NASA‚Äôs Black Marble Night Lights dataset shows how major metro areas such as Dallas - Fort Worth, Houston, and Austin maintain high light levels through the heat, but the sustainability of this energy use is questionable, as heat waves will continue to be a threat every summer in Texas.
  </Prose>

    ## Moving Forward: Building a Resilient Grid
    The future of energy in Texas hinges on preparing for both cold and hot extremes. After Winter Storm Uri, ERCOT and state regulators implemented limited reforms, including mandatory winterization of some infrastructure and increased communication protocols. However, experts argue that much more needs to be done.

    Diversifying energy sources will be critical, especially by expanding battery storage, grid interconnectivity, and demand response systems. Enhanced real-time monitoring using satellite remote sensing and ground observations can also provide early warning for periods of peak stress. Further, integrating renewable energy like wind and solar with resilient backup systems can help balance supply and demand during volatile weather conditions. Without significant investment and modernization, the risk of future blackouts remains.
  </Prose>
</Block>

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ## Conclusion
    The events of recent years have highlighted the operational limits of the ERCOT grid under extreme weather conditions, continuing to direct the public‚Äôs attention to the importance of a reliable and flexible power infrastructure. Texas‚Äôs energy system must be equipped to handle peak demand, regardless of season, without risking large-scale blackouts or cascading failures. Advances in satellite remote sensing and ground observational monitoring, grid forecasting, and diversified energy strategies can support better decision-making and emergency response. By learning from past disruptions and investing in forward-looking solutions, ERCOT can continue to serve the needs of Texas‚Äôs residents, industries, and communities.
  </Prose>
